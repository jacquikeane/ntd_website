Neglected Tropical Disease

The aim of this website is to explain why Neglected Tropical Diseases (NTDs) are so important to us all and why they are so fascinating to us as genome scientists. We want to bring together communities of people who are working on them, involved with work affected by them or just interested in finding out more about them and our efforts to decode the genomes of these organisms.

What are NTDs and why are we interested in them?

Neglected Tropical Diseases (NTDs) are a diverse group of infectious diseases caused by pathogens, including parasitic worms, protozoa, bacteria and viruses. By definition they cause a huge burden of disease (>numbers) and yet almost no money (define money) is spent on understanding their biology and/or combating the diseases they cause.

Why are NTDs important?

The diseases affect over 1 billion people across the world. They affect people living in regions of poverty and poor sanitation in tropical and subtropical regions. The infections are not as deadly as some other diseases, such as malaria, HIV and tuberculosis, but they do cause a wide range of health problems that in many cases have added social and economic implications.

Why are they neglected if they’re so important?

Because they mostly affect people in developing countries. Because despite there being, in some cases, interventions or treatments that can treat them, the communities affected don’t easily have access to them.

Why are we interested in studying them?

As well as wanting to be able to do something about helping the communities whose lives are affected by NTDs, they are also really caused by truly fascinating creatures - parasitic worms, protozoan parasites, bacteria and viruses.

Who are we and what are we doing about NTDs?

We are a group of research scientists from the Wellcome Trust Sanger Institute as well as collaborators around the world who are researching the biology of the parasites and bacteria that cause NTDs. We want to find out how they spread, how they interact with us and our tissues. We are doing this by studying their genomes. We hope that our research will help towards reducing NTDs: either by identifying weaknesses in the pathogens’ armour that can be exploited with drugs and vaccines, or simply by understanding more about how people can avoid becoming infected.

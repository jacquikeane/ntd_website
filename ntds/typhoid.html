Typhoid

Typhoid is caused by the bacteria Salmonella Typhi (official name: Salmonella enterica subspecies enterica, serovar Typhi). Salmonella are Gram negative rod-shaped bacteria belonging to the enterobacteriaceae family that also includes E. coli and Shigella.

Salmonella includes many different subtypes that vary in terms of host range and the intensity of the disease they induce. Many Salmonella are able to infect a wide range of animals and cause self-limiting gastroenteritis and diarrhoea. These are called non-typhoidal Salmonella and are sometimes responsible for food poisoning outbreaks. S. Typhi and Paratyphi A, however, have evolved into specialized strains capable of causing severe disease, but are limited to a single host â€“ humans.

What is the disease?

The most common symptom of typhoid is a high fever. Various other symptoms may include abdominal pain, headache, diarrhoea, constipation, loss of appetite, chills, fatigue, joint pain, coughing, vomiting, and rapid pulse. Rose spots on the chest are specific to typhoid, but only appear in some cases. If untreated, typhoid can progress to complications such as intestinal perforation and even death. Antibiotics are able to clear the bacterial infection, but antibiotic resistance is on the rise.

Who does it affect?

Typhoid affects nearly 12 million people a year, resulting in over 128,000 deaths. Typhoid disproportionately affects young children and adolescents aged 2 to 15 years in areas with poor access to clean water, sanitation and hygiene. Typhoid only infects humans and there is no animal or environmental reservoir. Typhoid is endemic across much of Asia, Africa, and parts of South America and Oceania.

Life cycle / how is it spread?

S. Typhi is shed in the stool by infected humans. In areas without sewers or water sanitation, faecal matter can be present in the water which is then consumed by others. After the S. Typhi enters the digestive system, it passes to the gut where it crosses the intestinal barrier and is disseminated throughout the body.

How can we hope to prevent-stop the disease?

Illness caused by typhoid can be reduced through an integrated prevention and treatment strategy including vaccines, dependable typhoid diagnostics, appropriate antibiotics, and improvements in clean water, sanitation and hygiene.

How do we use pathogen material to do research?

We are engaged in experiments to better understand how the bacteria invades the host and interacts with the immune system. We have analysed a global collection of 1,832 S. Typhi samples collected from 63 countries over the last century. As of 2017, this is the largest collection to date which enabled an unprecedented level of understanding of S. Typhi on a global level.

Analysing samples of S. Typhi allowed us to compare the strains and determine how they differ from region to region and change over time. We also constructed a family tree (phylogeny), inferred transmission events between regions, and documented the spread of a dominant multi-drug resistant (MDR) strain (Wong V.K. et al. 2015).

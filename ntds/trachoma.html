Trachoma

The pathogen Chlamydia trachomatis, infamous for being the infectious agent for bacterial sexually transmitted disease, is probably less well known as one of the leading causes of infectious blindness worldwide.

C. trachomatis is an obligate intracellular bacterium as it has to live inside a host cell. It displays two distinct phases during its life cycle (biphasic). During the first phase, it infects the host cells in a structure called an elementary body. Once inside the cell, the bacteria reside within an intra-cellular structure delimited by a double membrane known as an inclusion. This is where they replicate into larger particles (called reticular bodies), the second form of C. trachomatis. Eventually, the host cells burst open (lyse) and new C. trachomatis elementary bodies are released. These infect other nearby host cells or they get transmitted by contact.

Who it affects

Trachoma, the eye infection caused by C. trachomatis, is prevalent in 51 countries throughout the globe, specifically affecting remote areas and underprivileged populations. Twenty-one million people currently suffer from active trachoma, of which 1.2 million are totally blind as a consequence.

How it is spread

C. trachomatis serovars A, B and C cause the eye infection disease and are transmitted through contact with flies. Transmission is facilitated by suboptimal hygiene conditions.

Stopping the disease

Efforts towards elimination of trachoma focus on treatment through mass drug administration and on prevention such as water, sanitation and hygiene campaigns. These strategies have been responsible for a sharp decrease in trachoma prevalence over the past years.

Using pathogen material to do research

Next Generation Sequencing has revolutionized the study of C. trachomatis, an organism considered difficult to study in vitro due to its complex development cycle. Analysing the diversity of the C. trachomatis genome has led us to reveal several unanticipated characteristics of the evolution of this pathogen and helped us to understand some key characteristics of its biology.

One of our main research breakthroughs at the a href="http://www.sanger.ac.uk" target="_blank">Wellcome Sanger Institute (WSI) has been to highlight the extent and patterns of recombination that has been happening throughout the genome evolution of C. trachomatis. Within the genome of C. trachomatis bacteria that cause trachoma we find genome sequences from C. trachomatis bacteria that cause STDs. Therefore, exchange of genetic material is possible between these two strains, and may be responsible for the high diversity seen in C. trachomatis bacterial populations.

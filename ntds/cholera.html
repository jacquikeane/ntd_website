Cholera

Cholera is a life-threatening diarrhoea, characteristically causing ‘rice water’ stool in patients. It is caused by Vibrio cholerae, a Gram-negative bacterial pathogen. Infection with toxigenic V. cholerae is not itself dangerous; it is the loss of fluids and electrolytes, resulting from the presence of ‘cholera toxin’, that leads to death. Although this bacterial species is very diverse, causing a wide range of diarrhoeal illness, only one discrete subtype of V. cholerae causes the cholera pandemics that spread around the world.

Who it affects?

Cholera can affect people anywhere in the world, but it is a particular problem in areas that have suffered natural disasters. The sudden disruption to sanitation infrastructure caused by natural disasters can lead to contamination of drinking water with V. cholerae. If water containing this bacterium is ingested, the disease can take hold. Cholera patients shed millions of bacteria per millilitre of rice-water stool, making the disease highly contagious once it breaks out. Since cholera kills by dehydration, if there is no clean water available to rehydrate patients, the disease continues to spread and cause fatalities.

How is it spread?

Cholera is spread by the faecal-oral route, predominantly by consuming water that is contaminated with V. cholerae. An individual suffering from cholera will shed bacteria in their faeces for up to seven days after they stop having symptoms.

Stopping the disease

Cholera is entirely preventable. Generally, cholera breaks out when sanitation infrastructure is disrupted or destroyed. Providing clean water and good quality sanitation are the most effective ways to stop this disease. Oral rehydration of infected people is an inexpensive and effective treatment, and can be used in combination with antibiotic therapy to limit the duration of very severe infections. There are vaccines available to prevent cholera, but they are only partially effective.

Using pathogen material to do research

At WTSI, we are using the genome sequences of V. cholerae genomes to understand how different outbreaks of cholera are related to one another. This has allowed us to move toward explaining how this disease is moving across the planet. We are also beginning experimental work with V. cholerae to understand what it is that makes specific lineages of V. cholerae capable of causing global epidemics of disease, and what prevents other populations of this bacterium from doing so.

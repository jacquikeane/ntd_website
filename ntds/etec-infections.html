ETEC infections

Escherichia coli (E. coli) usually resides in the intestines of humans and animals. Most E. coli bacteria are harmless and form part of our gut flora, however some can cause disease, such as Enterotoxigenic E. coli (ETEC). Once ingested, ETEC attach to the surface of intestinal cells where the bacteria produce and secrete toxins into the cells of the intestine, causing production of fluid and, ultimately, diarrhoea.

ETEC infection causes a vast amount of watery diarrhoea to be expelled in addition to abdominal cramps. Symptoms can include fever, nausea, loss of appetite and headaches.

Who it affects

ETEC infection is the most common cause of bacterial diarrhea in children in low and middle-income countries. It is also the most common cause of travelersâ€™ diarrhea.

How it is spread

Enterotoxigenic E. coli is spread via food and water that has been contaminated with human or animal faeces.

Stopping the disease

To date there are no licensed vaccines for ETEC disease. Treatment includes oral rehydration therapy and antibiotics, although antibiotic resistance is becoming a problem. Improving sanitation and hygiene practices, such as hand washing, will also reduce the spread of disease.

Using pathogen material to do research

In this project, we re-direct human stem cells derived from skin cells into complex 3D small intestinal structures called organoids. These organoids have been shown to possess all cell types that are present in small intestinal tissue such as enterocytes, goblet cells, enteroendocrine cells and Paneth cells, as well as possessing microvilli and crypt structures.

We microinject the organoids with ETEC and look at the host (organoid cells) response to the infection to try to understand in more detail what is happening in the early stages of infection in the human intestine.

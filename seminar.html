Host-microbes interactions at mucosal surfaces: what can we learn from microbial parasites?

Professor Robert Hirt

Newcastle University

Research Interests

I use microbial parasites as model systems to study eukaryote cell and genome evolution. This work also leads to new insights into the molecular basis of host-parasite interactions. My research focus on the extracelluar parasite Trichomonas vaginalis and the intracellular parasites Microsporidia. Topics include the role of lateral gene transfer and gene duplications in parasite genome evolution, surface proteins mediating host-parasite interactions and mucosal microbiology including the microbiota.

Aims

  • Provide a better understanding of eukaryote origins and diversification through comparative genomics and molecular phylogenetics
  • Broaden comparative molecular and cellular studies of eukaryotes to provide a more comprehensive picture of their condition
  • Contribute to deciphering the molecular and cellular basis of mucosal microbes interactions with their hosts, integrating both pathogens (microbial parasites) and mutualists (including bacteria)

Mini biography

I initiated my career as a biologist with a PhD in cell biology studying polarised membrane trafficking in mammalian epithelial cells (University of Lausanne, Switzerland). In particular, I investigated the role of phosphorylation of the polymeric immunoglobulin receptor (pIgR) in the transcytosis of its ligand the dimeric IgA, both key molecules of the mucosal immune system. Being fascinated by evolutionary biology and recognising the power of comparative approaches and evolutionary biology to provide general insights into the molecular and cellular biology or eukaryotic cells, I joined the Natural History Museum (London) for a NERC-funded postdoctoral project on ciliate molecular evolution. My work provided novel insights into ciliate phylogeny and allowed me to clearly polarise the evolution of nuclear dualism in this group. My newly gained skills in evolutionary molecular cell biology equipped me to compete successfully for a Wellcome Trust “Career Development Fellowship" and then a Wellcome Trust “University Award” (five years funding). My research program since then has focused on the molecular cell biology and evolution of key human parasites focusing on Trichomonas vaginalis and microsporidia as well as Entamoeba and Giardia.
